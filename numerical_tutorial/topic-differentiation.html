Numerical Differentiation

1. Introduction: What Is a Derivative and Why Compute It Numerically?

A derivative represents the instantaneous rate of change of a function at a given pointâ€”in other words, the slope of its graph:

\( f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h} \)

To compute the derivative analytically, we must know the derivative formula of the function. However, this is not always possible:

  • When the function is defined only through data points (e.g., in a table)
  • When the function is too complex to differentiate
  • When real-time computations are required in live systems

In such cases, numerical differentiation techniques are applied.

2. Numerical Differentiation Methods

Numerical differentiation approximates the derivative based on known values of \( f(x) \) at discrete intervals.

a) Forward Difference

\( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \)

  • The simplest method
  • Easy to implement
  • Lower accuracy due to higher approximation error
b) Backward Difference

\( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x) - f(x-h)}{h} \)

  • Similar in logic to the forward difference
  • Estimates the slope looking to the left of \( x \)
  • Useful when only past values are available
c) Central Difference

\( f'(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - f(x-h)}{2h} \)

  • More balanced and accurate
  • The average of forward and backward differences
  • Lower approximation error

3. Example Calculation: Trigonometric Function

Function: \( f(x) = \sin(x) \)
Point: \( x = \frac{\pi}{4} \)
Step size: \( h = 0.1 \)

Analytical Derivative:
\( f'(x) = \cos(x) = \cos\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) \approx 0.7071 \)

a) Forward difference:

\( \frac{\sin(\frac{\pi}{4} + 0.1) - \sin(\frac{\pi}{4})}{0.1} \approx \frac{0.7742 - 0.7071}{0.1} = 0.671 \)

b) Backward difference:

\( \frac{\sin(\frac{\pi}{4}) - \sin(\frac{\pi}{4} - 0.1)}{0.1} \approx \frac{0.7071 - 0.6329}{0.1} = 0.742 \)

c) Central difference:

\( \frac{\sin(\frac{\pi}{4} + 0.1) - \sin(\frac{\pi}{4} - 0.1)}{2 \cdot 0.1} = \frac{0.7742 - 0.6329}{0.2} = 0.7065 \)

As seen, the central difference yields the closest approximation to the true value.

4. Higher-Order Derivative Approximations

a) Second Derivative:

\( f''(x) \approx \frac{f(x+h) - 2f(x) + f(x-h)}{h^2} \)

This formula is frequently used in differential equation approximations (e.g., FEM, CFD).

b) Higher-Order Derivatives

More complex formulas are used for higher derivatives.

Example for the third derivative:
\( f'''(x) \approx \frac{f(x+2h) - 2f(x+h) + 2f(x-h) - f(x-2h)}{2h^3} \)

These are important in areas like fluid dynamics and vibration analysis.

5. Error, Accuracy, and Choosing "h"

In numerical differentiation, the step size \( h \) plays a crucial role:

Step Size (h) Advantage Disadvantage
Very small Closer to truth Higher rounding errors
Very large Numerical stability Lower approximation accuracy

Therefore, error analysis should be performed to choose an optimal \( h \).

6. Python Implementation Example

import numpy as np

f = lambda x: np.sin(x)
x = np.pi / 4
h = 0.1

forward = (f(x + h) - f(x)) / h
backward = (f(x) - f(x - h)) / h
central = (f(x + h) - f(x - h)) / (2 * h)
true_val = np.cos(x)

print("Forward Approx:", forward)
print("Backward Approx:", backward)
print("Central Approx:", central)
print("True Derivative:", true_val)

The output shows that the central difference provides the most accurate result.

8. Conclusion

Numerical differentiation becomes essential when function values are known, but their derivatives are not. With the right method and step size, approximation error can be minimized.

  • Forward/Backward differences: Quick but less accurate
  • Central difference: Preferred for higher accuracy
  • Higher derivatives: Useful in advanced engineering and modeling

Numerical differentiation is a foundational tool in fields like AI, signal processing, financial modeling, and more.
