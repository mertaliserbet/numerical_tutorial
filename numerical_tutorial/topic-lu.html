LU Decomposition

LU Decomposition is a method used to solve systems of linear equations by breaking down a square matrix \( A \) into the product of two simpler matrices:

\( A = LU \)

Where:

  • L is a lower triangular matrix (non-zero entries on and below the diagonal)
  • U is an upper triangular matrix (non-zero entries on and above the diagonal)

This decomposition allows us to simplify the solution of the system:

\( Ax = b \Rightarrow LUx = b \)

We solve this in two steps:

  • \( Ly = b \) â†’ Solve for y using forward substitution
  • \( Ux = y \) â†’ Solve for x using back substitution

Why Use LU Decomposition?

  • When solving multiple systems with the same A matrix but different b vectors, LU Decomposition is highly efficient.
  • Instead of repeating Gaussian Elimination each time, we decompose A once and reuse L and U for all b vectors.
  • It saves time and memory, and is especially useful in large-scale problems.

Step-by-Step Example

Given a system:

\( A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \)

We want to decompose it into:

\( A = LU = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ l_{21} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} \\ 0 & u_{22} \end{bmatrix} \)

Multiply the two matrices:

\( \begin{bmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} \\ l_{21} u_{11} & l_{21} u_{12} + u_{22} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 4 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \)

From this, we get:

  • \( u_{11} = 2 \)
  • \( u_{12} = 3 \)
  • \( l_{21} = 4 / 2 = 2 \)
  • \( u_{22} = 7 - (2 \cdot 3) = 1 \)

So:

\( L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad U = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

Solving \( Ax = b \)

Suppose:

\( b = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \)

Step 1: Solve \( Ly = b \)

\( \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \Rightarrow y_1 = 5, \, y_2 = 11 - 2 \cdot 5 = 1 \)

Step 2: Solve \( Ux = y \)

\( \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \Rightarrow x_2 = 1, \, x_1 = (5 - 3 \cdot 1)/2 = 1 \)

Final solution:

\( x = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \)

What About Pivoting?

In some cases, LU decomposition without row swapping fails. In those cases, we use LU Decomposition with Pivoting:

\( PA = LU \)

Where \( P \) is a permutation matrix that keeps track of row swaps.

Python Example (with SciPy)


import numpy as np
from scipy.linalg import lu

A = np.array([[2, 3],
              [4, 7]])

P, L, U = lu(A)

print("P =\n", P)
print("L =\n", L)
print("U =\n", U)

Advantages of LU Decomposition

  • Great for solving \( Ax = b \) with multiple right-hand sides \( b \)
  • More modular and reusable than Gaussian elimination
  • Forms the foundation of many iterative methods
  • Used in engineering, data science, finance, and more

Conclusion

LU Decomposition is a fundamental technique in Numerical Methods. It simplifies solving systems of equations, especially when you need to solve them repeatedly for different inputs. It's not only theoretically important but also practically powerful in many real-world applications.
